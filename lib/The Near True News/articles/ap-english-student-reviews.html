AP English Student Reviews Episode of CSI

September 3, 2014

By: Marcus Duquette

According to AP English student Charlotte Walker, Season 14 Episode 6 of Crime Scene Investigation made use of many important storytelling tools, painting a vivid picture of the investigation into the death of mail-order bride, Alexandra.

Charlotte thought the show touched on important philosophical ideas. “The victim’s husband and prime suspect appears visibly shaken by the news of his wife’s death when talking with investigators, but quickly moves on with his life. He has already given up hope that his wife might be alive and ordered another by the time the investigators arrive. This expertly showcases the essence of internal pluralism, a philosophical idea that’s been around since the 5th century B.C.E., where multiple contradictory opinions work against each other within the individual.”

According to Charlotte, the crisis felt by lead investigator Stokes alludes to the idea of existential hell. “I think everyone can relate to his confusion: is there a reason to catch these criminals? Why is human life sacred? Why am I here? CSI forces you to think about questions like these, and that is what makes it such a great show.”

Charlotte had some advice for other viewers. “I watched this with my boyfriend, but I tried to apply what I’ve been learning in AP English. I found that following the annotation guidelines from class really helped me to better understand this show. In English, we’re supposed to try to annotate something important on every page. I printed out each frame of the show – all 31,680 of them – and annotated for something new on every one. It was challenging for many of the frames, but, looking closely, you can detect a change in each. For example, on frame 19,389, I noticed an ant in the bottom left corner of the autopsy table. While an inexperienced viewer may have dismissed this as insignificant, I managed to catch the reference to the famous Aristotle quote, ‘Time crumbles everything: Everything grows old under the power of time, and is forgotten over the lapse of time.’ The ant was a symbol of time’s effects.”

“I also wrote a one paragraph summary of the plot at every commercial break,” Walker added, “as you would for chapters in a book.”

Charlotte concluded that episode 7 fell far short of the high bar set by episode 6, due to its overuse of foreshadowing. “Such a cliché,” she said, rolling her eyes. “We learned about foreshadowing in, like, seventh grade.”
